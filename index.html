Section I

Planning Challenge

The Challenge

The Planning Challenge
on St. John

St. John faces a persistent imbalance: land zoned exclusively residential, while community needs for attainable housing, local services, and economic access go unmet year after year.

  • Cruz Bay absorbs disproportionate development pressure
  • Locals face limited housing ownership and commercial opportunity
  • The same establishments that cater to tourists may not serve local needs
  • Exclusively residential zoning blocks thoughtful mixed-use solutions
Section I

Why This Proposal Exists

Our Motivation

Why This Proposal Exists

This isn't a luxury development. It's a direct response to what St. John's year-round community has long needed - rooted in place, respectful of culture, designed to serve people who already call this island home.

Attainable Housing

Apartments and residential units accessible to local families and workers - not just seasonal visitors or outside investors.

Local Commerce

A neighborhood strip mall serving everyday needs - groceries, pharmacy, services - for residents, not tourists.

Public Green Space

A dedicated public park woven into the development - shade, gathering, and breathing room for the whole neighborhood.

Growth Strategy

01 - A More Balanced
Growth Pattern

Rather than intensifying already-stressed nodes, this proposal distributes growth thoughtfully - building community where people already live, reducing pressure where it's most acute.

  • Reduces strain on Cruz Bay's over-burdened infrastructure
  • Supports distributed, walkable neighborhood nodes island-wide
  • Aligns with DPNR goals for sustainable, balanced development
  • Low-rise scale respects surrounding residential character
Housing Access

02 - Housing and Ownership
Opportunity

St. John's housing market is increasingly inaccessible to the people who sustain the island's community. This proposal prioritizes apartments that locals can actually own.

  • Attainably priced apartments for purchase by local families
  • Long-term leasing options for island workforce residents
  • Reduces reliance on off-island commuter or rental housing
Commercial Vision

03 - Local-Serving
Commercial Uses

The strip mall component is explicitly oriented around everyday resident needs - not tourism, not destination retail. Small footprints, local operators, neighborhood-scale services.

  • Grocery, pharmacy, or hardware store access
  • Locally owned small businesses and service providers
  • No large-format or national chain anchor tenants
  • Designed to reduce off-island trips for basic necessities
Economic Impact

04 Broader Local
Economic Participation

Development that benefits the broader island community not just investors. Local jobs, local operators, local ownership, local spending that stays on St. John.

  • Construction jobs for local tradespeople and contractors
  • Commercial leases structured to favor local business owners
  • Resident ownership builds community equity over time
  • Everyday spending recirculates within the island economy
  • Reduces economic leakage to off-island service providers
Community Life

05 Supporting Year-Round
Local Life

St. John is more than a destination it's a living community. This development is designed for the people who are here every day, not just when the ferries are full.

12 Months, Not 4

Services and housing that function for residents year-round, not just during peak tourist season when vendors disappear.

Neighbors, Not Guests

Residents who invest in the community, attend schools, use local services, and participate in civic island life.

Stay On-Island

Fewer ferry trips to St. Thomas for groceries, hardware, or basic services - everything residents need within reach.

Alternative Path

06 - Practical Alternative to
Higher-Pressure Areas

Without thoughtful spot zoning, development pressure defaults to the most sensitive and tourist-facing areas. This proposal offers a planned, community-first alternative.

  • Neighborhood-scale vs. resort-scale or high-density development
  • Integrated park and community space included by design - not optional
  • Reduces incentive for denser tourist-zone infill elsewhere
  • Planned, controlled mixed-use vs. unplanned or opportunistic development
Our Commitments

07 - Culturally Respectful &
Environmentally Conscious

St. John's character - its people, its landscape, its rhythm - is not a backdrop. It's the reason this proposal takes the form it does.

Native Planting

Landscaping uses native Caribbean species - drought-tolerant, habitat-supportive, and ecologically appropriate to the island.

Water Stewardship

Rainwater collection, permeable surfaces, and low-impact stormwater management are integrated into the site plan.

Cultural Sensitivity

Design language, materials, and scale are respectful of the island's architectural heritage and community identity.

Design Approach

08 · Neighborhood-Scale Character
& Compatibility

Everything about this proposal is calibrated to fit into — not stand out from — the existing residential fabric of the surrounding community.

  • Low-rise, 2–3 story maximum building heights throughout
  • Natural materials: wood, stone, painted stucco in earth tones
  • Design that doensn't disrupt, but blends in
Open Space

09 · Public Greenspace
& Livability

The public park is not an afterthought. It is a central feature of this proposal — a permanent, freely accessible gathering place for the entire community.

  • Publicly accessible and open to all St. John residents
  • Native shade trees, seating, and gathering areas throughout
  • Designed for everyday use: children, families, elders
  • Permeable landscaping supports groundwater recharge
  • Adds permanent green space to this part of the island
The Case for Flexibility

Why Zoning Flexibility
Is Justified

  • Community need is documented — housing access, local services, and economic opportunity are persistent, well-known gaps on St. John
  • Scale is appropriate — this is not large-format commercial; it is neighborhood-serving mixed-use at a modest footprint
  • Public benefit is direct — park space, attainable ownership, and local jobs are built into the program, not afterthoughts
  • Design is context-sensitive — low-rise, screened, and compatible with the existing residential setting on all sides
  • Alternatives are worse — without this outlet, pressure concentrates where it causes the most environmental and social harm
Our Ask

Request and Next Steps

We respectfully request that DPNR approve spot zoning for these parcels to permit mixed residential and commercial use — in recognition of the genuine, lasting community benefit this development delivers.

01

Spot Zone Approval

Mixed-use designation for the subject parcels, permitting residential apartments, neighborhood commercial, and public park.

02

Community Review

We welcome a public comment period and are committed to meaningful community engagement throughout the design and permitting process.

03

Formal Application

Upon approval in principle, we will submit full site plans, environmental review documents, and complete permitting applications.

Thank you for your consideration.
We believe in St. John's community — and this proposal is built on that belief.
